CASE 2: Technical Writing for Technical Audiences

[discussion]

 

I've written and proofed hundreds of pages of documentation for Microsoft corporation, In that time, I had to learn and adhere to strict style guides. I can tell you from experience, there is a reason why all Microsoft documentation has sterile qualities: localization. Microsoft documentation has to be translated into 70+ languages, so all documentation goes through a process called localization, wherein editors like myself detect, remove and rewrite such things as: passive constructions (some languages can't translate phrases without objects), subject and object pronouns (some languages have no equivalent of "it"), gerunds (Chinese variants in particular have no translation for verbs used as nouns) and, finally, Microsoft style guides mandate that all products be referred to by their full name at all times. For example, all "docs" are "Microsoft Word documents", and "servers" are always "Microsoft Server 2008 Enterprise servers".

 

This once led to me, in bug-testing, filing a bug that read as follows:

 

"Microsoft Operations Manager Management Server Manager Management Pack fails to update."

 

True story.

 

CASE 3: Creative Writing, Writing with Voice and Persuasive Argument

Submit a writing sample on the topic of how writing influences audience experience in training

 

[writing sample]

 

As it turns out, American English happens to have quite a bit of structure to it, and I'm not talking about when to use semi-colons, or em-dashes instead of en-dashes, or any of that sort of paint-by-numbers aspect of linguistic structure that makes it easy to apply rules and measures to proofing and editing text; I'm talking about the structure that makes a sentence flow instead of chop, permits the reader to focus on the content without struggling over the ungainly cracks that appear when the structure is broken by the forced fusion of two technically-correct-yet-aesthetically-jarring terms whose proximity and position has been either carelessly or ignorantly determined by an untrained operator of the language.

 

I don't care for extra words, either, especially ones that are used primarily to demonstrate vocabulary. To me, language is like a highly complex engine, and there's a correct word to use at a specific time and place -to address the exact audience and match their metaphorical and literal understanding of the topic-, just as there is a correct wrench to manipulate the hex nuts in the engine: yes, the 8mm bit fits the 5/16ths-inch lug, but it's off by a couple thousandths of an inch, and when it comes to language, most of us can sense when something is just a tiny bit off. We don't always know what's odd about it, why it catches in our mind and blocks up the flow of content, if just for an instant, but those funny turns of phrase, those misplaced commas, those odd capitalizations of words, they all create a bit of blockage at the intake of language. They interrupt our concentration when we are trying to learn, or trying to understand another's point of view.

 

And this is why writing and editing can make and break an audience's experience with a learning tool. If the structure of the language is flawed and disturbs the smooth transition of content from writer to reader, the reader must work harder to learn. That extra work is the difference between focus and distraction, between comprehension and confusion, and between a successful training document and a failed one.
